The Internet (or internet) is the global system of interconnected computer networks that uses the Internet protocol suite (TCP/IP) to communicate between networks and devices. It is a network of networks that consists of private, public, academic, business, and government networks of local to global scope, linked by a broad array of electronic, wireless, and optical networking technologies.[1]

The Internet carries a vast range of information resources and services, such as the inter-linked hypertext documents and applications of the World Wide Web (WWW), electronic mail, telephony, and file sharing.[2]

History

The origins of the Internet date back to the development of packet switching and research commissioned by the United States Department of Defense in the 1960s to enable time-sharing of computers.[3] The primary precursor network, the ARPANET, initially served as a backbone for interconnection of regional academic and military networks in the 1980s.

The funding of the National Science Foundation Network as a new backbone in the 1980s, as well as private funding for other commercial extensions, led to worldwide participation in the development of new networking technologies, and the merger of many networks. The term "internet" was popularized in 1974 by Vint Cerf and Bob Kahn in their landmark paper on TCP/IP.[4]

TCP/IP and the modern Internet

On January 1, 1983, ARPANET transitioned to TCP/IP, and from that date, researchers began to assemble the "network of networks" that became the modern Internet. The term "internet" came to refer specifically to this global collection of networks.[5]

Infrastructure

The Internet structure and its usage characteristics have been studied extensively and the possibility of usage charges has been eliminated. Instead, there has been a focus on providing a fast, secure, and reliable connection to as many users as possible.[6]

  • Internet exchange points (IXPs) connect major networks
  • Submarine communications cables carry most transoceanic traffic
  • Internet service providers (ISPs) provide access to end users
  • Content delivery networks (CDNs) distribute data globally
